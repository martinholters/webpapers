The non-iterative method revisited

Martin Holters

Introduction

In [1], Ducceschi and Bilbao have introduced a family of non-iterative discretization schemes for ODEs. However, in their presentation, they make several assumptions regarding the structure of the ODE, limiting the gerenality of these schemes. Here, we show that for the second-order accurate variant in particular, the method is applicable to general (potentially vector-valued) ODEs.

In the following, x(t)x(t)x(t) denotes the state of a system goverened by an ODE and x˙(t)\dot{x}(t)x˙(t) it derivative. When obvious from the context, we drop the argument ttt. We seek an approximation x^(n)≈x(nT)\hat{x}(n) \approx x(nT)x^(n)≈x(nT) where TTT is the sampling interval. For given signals like an input u(t)u(t)u(t), we likewise denote samples as e.g. u^(n)=u(nT)\hat{u}(n)=u(nT)u^(n)=u(nT).

Connection to the implicit midpoint method

If we apply the the implicit midpoint method to (20) and approximate c((n+12)T)c((n+\tfrac{1}{2})T)c((n+21​)T) with 12(c^(n+1)+c^(n))\tfrac{1}{2}(\hat{c}(n+1)+\hat{c}(n))21​(c^(n+1)+c^(n)) [2], we obtain

So if we terminate the Newton-Raphson method after the first iteration and let x^(n+1)=x^1\hat{x}(n+1)=\hat{x}_1x^(n+1)=x^1​, we recover (21). Thus, the non-iterative method of accuracy order 2 can also be viewed as an approximation of the implicit midpoint method with (very) early termination of the numerical solver. It is remarkable that even with an exact solution, the implicit midpoint method has accuracy order 2, so this approximation does not decrease the accuracy order (but it does impact stability).
